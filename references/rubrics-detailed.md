# Detailed Scoring Rubrics

Use these expanded rubrics when doing deep analysis. The compact version in SKILL.md is for quick scoring.

## Substance (Evidence Quality)

| Score | Description | Example |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| 1 | Generic platitude, no evidence | "I'm a great collaborator" |
| 2 | Vague claim with weak support | "I improved the process and people liked it" |
| 3 | Specific claim, missing quantification | "I redesigned the onboarding flow and reduced drop-off" |
| 4 | Quantified with context, missing alternatives considered | "I redesigned onboarding, reducing drop-off from 40% to 25%" |
| 5 | Quantified + alternatives weighed + decision rationale + outcome | "I redesigned onboarding after testing 3 approaches. We chose progressive disclosure over a wizard because our data showed users abandoned at step 3. Drop-off fell from 40% to 25%, validated over 10K users." |

**Coaching notes:**
- Push for numbers even when "hard to measure" — approximations with caveats are better than none
- Ask: "What would a skeptic say is missing from this evidence?"
- Flag when impact is claimed without explaining the candidate's specific contribution

**Root causes when stuck at 1-2:**
- Candidate hasn't done the reflection work — they know what happened but haven't extracted what mattered
- Conflict avoidance: stripping stories of tension, stakes, and difficulty makes evidence disappear
- Impostor syndrome: downplaying real impact because they feel they don't deserve the credit

**Root causes when stuck at 3:**
- "Good enough" syndrome — candidate has specifics but stops before quantifying
- Hasn't thought about alternatives considered
- Missing the "so what" — evidence exists but isn't connected to business impact

## Structure (Narrative Clarity)

| Score | Description | Example |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| 1 | Stream of consciousness, no clear point | Jumps between topics, listener lost |
| 2 | Central idea unclear until the end | Buries the lead, meanders to point |
| 3 | Clear structure but missing transitions | Good STAR but choppy between sections |
| 4 | Well-structured with smooth flow, minor tangents | Clean arc with one or two detours |
| 5 | Crisp structure: setup → conflict → resolution → impact | Every sentence advances the story, lands with clear takeaway |

**Coaching notes:**
- Best answers front-load the headline: "The key learning was X. Here's how we got there..."
- Tangents often signal the candidate is unsure what matters
- Practice the "30-second version" to force clarity on what's essential

**Root causes when stuck at 1-2:**
- Narrative hoarding: trying to cram everything into one answer
- No mental model for story structure
- Anxiety-driven stream of consciousness

**Root causes when stuck at 3:**
- Knows STAR but applies it mechanically — transitions feel forced
- Hasn't practiced at multiple time constraints

## Relevance (Question Fit)

| Score | Description | Example |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| 1 | Doesn't address the question asked | Asked about conflict, answers about a successful launch |
| 2 | Tangentially related, misses core | Asked about failure, talks about a challenge they overcame easily |
| 3 | Addresses question but includes irrelevant details | Right story but 40% is background noise |
| 4 | Directly addresses question with minor drift | Solid answer with one unnecessary tangent |
| 5 | Laser-focused, every sentence serves the answer | Could not remove a sentence without losing value |

**Coaching notes:**
- Restate the question before answering to ensure alignment
- Common failure: using a "favorite" story that doesn't quite fit
- Ask: "If the interviewer only remembers one thing, what should it be?"

**Root causes when stuck at 1-2:**
- Can't identify the core of a question — defaults to a topic story regardless of what the question targets
- Story-first thinking: starts from "which story do I want to tell?" instead of "what is this question asking?"

**Root causes when stuck at 3:**
- Right story, wrong framing — 30-40% of the answer doesn't serve the question
- No internal "am I still answering what they asked?" monitor

## Credibility (Believability)

| Score | Description | Example |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| 1 | Claims with no support or obvious exaggeration | "I single-handedly transformed the company culture" |
| 2 | Support is vague or generic | "I increased efficiency" |
| 3 | Specific details but missing numbers or outcomes | "I built a dashboard the team used daily" |
| 4 | Quantified with context, could use stronger proof | "Dashboard reduced reporting time by 50%" |
| 5 | Numbers + artifacts + validation + realistic constraints | "Dashboard cut reporting from 4h to 2h (verified by team survey). CEO mentioned it in all-hands. Took 3 weeks with one engineer." |

**Coaching notes:**
- Credibility increases when candidate acknowledges constraints and trade-offs
- Third-party validation strengthens claims
- Watch for "we" vs "I" confusion
- Realistic timelines and resource constraints make stories more believable

**Root causes when stuck at 1-2:**
- Over-claiming / status anxiety: inflating contributions (reduces credibility)
- Reflexive "we" framing: obscures individual contribution
- Fabrication: red flag, address directly

**Root causes when stuck at 3:**
- Has details but doesn't package them as proof
- Missing third-party signals: never quotes what others said about the work

## Differentiation (Uniqueness)

| Score | Description | Example |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| 1 | Generic answer any prepared candidate could give | "I used data to make better decisions" |
| 2 | Some specificity but relies on common frameworks | "I applied the RICE framework to prioritize" |
| 3 | Real details but no earned insight or defensible POV | "I built a dashboard that saved 10 hours/week" |
| 4 | Includes earned secrets or spiky POV; sounds like a specific person | "I learned that the fastest way to kill a feature is to let it succeed at small scale — it becomes politically unkillable before you have real data" |
| 5 | Unmistakably this candidate — earned secrets + defensible stance + unique framing | "After launching 12 features in 2 years, I developed a 'kill threshold' system. If a feature doesn't hit 40% adoption in 6 weeks, I kill it regardless of stakeholder enthusiasm. Three of those kills freed resources for our actual breakout product." |

**Coaching notes:**
- Differentiation separates Hire from Strong Hire
- The biggest enemy is AI-polished prep — sounds "correct" but could be anyone's
- Push: "What do you know from this experience that most people wouldn't know?"
- Spiky POVs must be defensible, authentic, and backed by experience

**Root causes when stuck at 1-2:**
- Over-reliance on frameworks and best practices
- Fear of being wrong: choosing "safe" over distinctive
- Haven't done the reflection work to extract what's unique

**Root causes when stuck at 3:**
- Has real experience but presents it as straightforward execution
- Doesn't realize what makes their perspective unusual
- Missing the opinionated interpretation

## Root Cause Taxonomy (Cross-Dimensional)

| Root Cause | How It Manifests | Affected Dimensions | Targeted Fix |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Can't identify question core** | Answers miss the point | Relevance, Structure | Question-decoding drills |
| **Reflexive "we" framing** | Individual contribution unclear | Credibility, Substance | I/we audit |
| **Conflict avoidance** | Stories lack tension and stakes | Substance, Differentiation | Tension-mining |
| **Status anxiety** | Inflated claims interviewers don't believe | Credibility, Differentiation | Constraint practice |
| **Narrative hoarding** | Answers run long, structure collapses | Structure, Relevance | Constraint ladder drill |
| **Fear of being wrong** | Generic, safe answers | Differentiation, Substance | Spiky POV practice |
| **Performance stress** | Structure breaks, retrieval fails | Structure, all | Psychological readiness |
| **Cultural communication style** | Indirect framing, modesty | Credibility, Structure, Substance | Adaptation coaching |
| **Linguistic formality** | Overly formal, idiom gaps | Differentiation, Credibility | Gentle register calibration |

When the same root cause appears across 2+ sessions, escalate to Active Coaching Strategy. Prescribe ONE intervention targeting the root cause, not separate drills for each dimension.

## Seniority Calibration Details

Always state which calibration band you're using when scoring.

- **Early career (0-3 years)**: A "4 on Substance" means specific examples with at least one metric. Differentiation can come from learning velocity and intellectual curiosity.
- **Mid-career (4-8 years)**: A "4 on Substance" means quantified impact with alternatives considered. Differentiation requires genuine earned secrets from hands-on work.
- **Senior/Lead (8-15 years)**: A "4 on Substance" means systems-level thinking — second-order effects, organizational impact.
- **Executive (15+ years)**: A "4 on Substance" means business-level impact with P&L awareness. Differentiation requires coherent leadership philosophy.

## Trend Analysis

Track average scores per dimension over time:
- **Improving**: +0.5 or more from baseline
- **Stagnant**: Within ±0.3 of baseline
- **Declining**: -0.5 or more from baseline

Stagnant scores after 3+ sessions signal need to change approach, not just practice more.
